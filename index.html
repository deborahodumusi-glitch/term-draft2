Hypothesis: Increased late-night screen exposure reduces sleep quality

Plots Explores the relationship between screen time before bed and sleep quality, with mental well-being as the third variable visualized through color coding.


Overview of My Scatter Plots My visualizations showed that increased screentime before bed was generally linked to lower sleep duration and quality, though some data points deviated from this trend. I also included variables such as exercise, bedtime routines, sleep start and finish times, screentime activities, restfulness, mental wellbeing, and their relationships with an 8-hour sleep standard. Using color and bubble size to represent mental wellbeing and restfulness clarified that nights with higher scores in these areas often had better sleep outcomes than screentime alone suggested. This indicates that while screentime impacts sleep, emotional wellbeing and restfulness play key roles, so improving sleep likely requires attention to both digital habits and overall mental state.

Key Observations in the Data Collected

In my visualizations, I observed a general decline in sleep duration and sleep quality as screentime before bed increased, although several points fell outside this pattern. Adding mental wellbeing and restfulness as color and bubble-size indicators helped explain these deviations, since nights with higher wellbeing or restfulness sometimes produced better sleep outcomes than the screentime alone would predict. Overall, the data suggests that while screentime influences sleep, variations in wellbeing and restfulness also shape these results, showing that sleep depends on more than one factor.
